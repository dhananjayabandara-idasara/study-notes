Unit 1

Plan the Work, Work the Plan

This unit is about organising yourself and managing time. The grammar you learn here: reflexive pronouns and countable nouns. You also practise two English sounds and write a letter to a pen friend.

1

Reflexive pronounsmyself · yourself · himself · herself · itself · ourselves · yourselves · themselves

▾
1Let's get into it
A small word that means "no help!" You're at the kade and a friend asks, "Who fixed your bicycle?" You grin and say, "I fixed it myself!" That little word — myself — proudly says you did it with nobody's help. It bounces the action straight back to you. Words like this are called reflexive pronouns, and Unit 1 is full of them (in the very first dialogue: "make yourselves comfortable").
2In plain words

A reflexive pronoun is the word you use when the same person both does the action and receives it. It always ends in:

• -self when it is one person/thing (myself, yourself, himself…)
• -selves when it is more than one (ourselves, yourselves, themselves)

Every subject pronoun has its own matching reflexive. Learn them as pairs (Pupil's Book, p.3):

Subject (singular)ReflexiveSubject (plural)Reflexive
Imyselfweourselves
youyourselfyou (all)yourselves
hehimselftheythemselves
sheherself
ititself
3See it
4Worked examples + your turn

The Pupil's Book (Learning Point, p.4) gives reflexive pronouns two jobs:

Job 1 — as the object (right after a verb or a preposition)

The kitten cleaned itself.  (verb "cleaned" + reflexive)

We can be proud of ourselves.  (preposition "of" + reflexive)

Job 2 — for emphasis (to stress who did it)

Our art teacher himself designed the invitation.  (= nobody else — the teacher personally)

Our English teacher said that she herself would prepare them.

✍️ Your turn (Activity 1.3 style): add the correct reflexive pronoun.

1) I bought ______ a dictionary.   2) He made ______ a cup of tea.   3) They arranged ______ in a circle.   4) My father grows these vegetables ______ .

1) myself   2) himself   3) themselves   4) himself (emphasis).
Trick: match the doer — I→myself, He→himself, They→themselves.
The selfish pencil only ever talked about itself. The twins next to it? They only talked about themselves!
5Watch out!
Common mistakes
  • "hisself" and "theirselves" are NOT real words. The correct forms are himself and themselves.
  • Don't use a reflexive when there is no bounce-back. Myself went home. → I went home.
  • One person → -self; two or more → -selves. "You and your sister, behave yourselves!"
6Quick self-check
🪞 Try it — tap a subject to see its reflexive pronoun
Tap a subject above…
Now think, then reveal:
1. "The children behaved ______ at the temple."
More than one child → themselves. ("theirselves" is not a real word.)
2. Correct or wrong? "Myself made the poster."
Wrong. Say "I made the poster myself." A reflexive can't be the subject.
7Remember this
🪃
"-self for one, -selves for more — the action bounces back to the door it came from." If the doer and the receiver are the same, reach for a reflexive pronoun.
2

Countable (count) nounssingular & plural forms · spelling rules

▾
1Let's get into it
At the pola (market)… You can say "give me three mangoes" — you can count them one by one. But you can't say "three rices" or "two waters." Some nouns you can count, and those are the stars of this box.
2In plain words

A countable noun (count noun) is a thing that has a singular form (one) and a plural form (many). In the singular it usually needs a small word in front — a determiner such as a, an, the, one, this.

Pupil's Book (p.8): "A count noun is a noun which has a singular and a plural form. It is usually used after a determiner in the singular form." Examples: a book → books, a bus → buses, a mango → mangoes.

To make the plural, follow these spelling rules:

If the word ends in…Do thisExamples (Activity 1.7)

-s, -ss, -sh, -ch, -x (a hissing sound)add -esbus → buses · box → boxes · compass → compasses · torch → torches · toothbrush → toothbrushes
consonant + oadd -estomato → tomatoes · mango → mangoes

4Worked example + your turn
Worked

"a torch" → why torches? Because it ends in -ch (a hiss), so we add -es.

"a tomato" → ends in consonant + o, so add -es → tomatoes.

✍️ Your turn (Activity 1.7): write the plural of — 1) a vase   2) a compass   3) a uniform   4) a mango

1) vases (just -s)   2) compasses (ends in -ss)   3) uniforms (just -s)   4) mangoes (consonant + o).
5Watch out!
Common mistakes
  • A singular count noun can't stand alone — say a book, not just "book." (I bought book.)
  • -o words are tricky: tomato, mango, potato take -es, but photo, piano take only -s. Learn the common ones.
  • Spelling traps: bus → buses (not "buss"); box → boxes (not "boxs").
6Quick self-check
1. Plural of "a bus", "a mango", "a city"?
buses (hiss → -es) · mangoes (consonant+o → -es) · cities (consonant+y → -ies).
2. Fix it: "She packed two box and three cloth."
"She packed two boxes and three cloths." (box → boxes; cloth → cloths)
7Remember this
🐍
"Hiss & -o, add -es to go." Words ending in a hissing sound (s/ss/sh/ch/x) or in consonant+o take -es. Most other words just take -s.
3

Pronunciation: the /pl/ and /pr/ soundsconsonant blends · Workbook Activity 04

▾
1Let's get into it
"play" or "pray"? Say play, then say pray. Feel your tongue move? Mixing up /pl/ and /pr/ can turn "I want to play" into "I want to pray" — two very different things! These two blends appear all over Unit 1 (plan, prepare, prize…), so let's get them crisp.
2In plain words

Both blends start with a quick /p/ and then glide straight into the next sound — with no vowel squeezed in between.

• /pl/ — after the /p/, your tongue touches just behind your top teeth (the "l" position). Smile a little: plan.
• /pr/ — after the /p/, your tongue curls slightly back (the "r" position): prince.

3See it — sort the words
/pl/  (tongue flat, behind teeth)/pr/  (tongue curled back)
plan · play · plant · plug · plane · platform · plastic · plural · pleasure · place · plumber* prince · prepare · protect · proud · price · present · promise · pray · printer · principal · prize

*In plumber the b is silent — say "PLUM-er." (Workbook Activity 04, p.4.)

4Worked example + your turn
Worked

platform → /pl/ (tongue flat).   promise → /pr/ (tongue curled).

✍️ Your turn: /pl/ or /pr/ ? — 1) please   2) prize   3) platoon   4) priest   5) plug

1) /pl/ please   2) /pr/ prize   3) /pl/ platoon   4) /pr/ priest   5) /pl/ plug.
5Watch out!
Common mistakes
  • Don't add an extra vowel: it's "plate", not "pu-late," and "pray", not "pu-ray." Keep /p/ and the next sound glued together.
  • Listen for the tiny difference — play / pray, plum / prom. If a listener is confused, slow down and feel your tongue.
6Quick self-check
1. Sort: protect · plastic · proud · plan
/pl/: plastic, plan   ·   /pr/: protect, proud.
2. Which word has /pr/ — "plough" or "proud"?
proud (/pr/). "plough" is /pl/.
7Remember this
👅
"PL = pLay, tongue flat. PR = pRoud, tongue curled." Smile for /pl/, growl a little for /pr/ — and never slip a vowel in between.

Writing: a letter to a pen friend (& a list)informal letter format · Activity 1.9

▾
1Let's get into it
A friend across the world is waiting to hear from you. In Unit 1, Amitha writes to her pen friend Svetlana in Russia. An informal letter like this is easy marks in the exam — if you keep the simple format. Let's learn the shape, then fill it with your own news.
2In plain words

An informal letter has five parts, always in this order:

1) Your address + date (top right)
2) Greeting — "Dear ___,"
3) Opening line — a friendly thank-you or hope
4) Body — your news, and a question back to your friend
5) Closing — "Love," / "Your friend," + your name

3See it — the skeleton
5, Vijay Road,
Puttalam, Sri Lanka.
20th February 20..

Dear Svetlana,

(opening) Thank you very much for your letter and the lovely photographs. Hope everything is going well at your end.

(body — your news) I was a little busy last week, so I prepared a plan to finish all my school work on time. This month our Nature Club organised a beach clean-up.

(question back) By the way, what are your plans for this year?

(closing) Hope to hear from you soon.
Love,
Amitha

4Worked + your turn

Unit 1 also asks for a list of items (the bag-packing task, p.6). A list is short, one item per line, no full sentences: uniforms, toothbrush, soap, torch, water bottle…

✍️ Your turn: write 6–8 sentences to a pen friend asking about their plans for the year. Sneak in one reflexive pronoun and one plural count noun from this unit.

"Dear Nimal, thank you for your letter. Last month our class organised a book sale — we planned it ourselves! We sold many books and two boxes of old magazines. What are your plans for this year? Hope to hear from you soon. Your friend, Kasun."
5Watch out!
Common mistakes
  • Don't forget the comma after the greeting: "Dear Svetlana," and after the closing: "Love,".
  • An informal letter is friendly — use short, warm sentences, not stiff formal English.
  • Always ask a question back — it shows you care and gives your friend something to reply to.
6Quick self-check
1. Where do the address and date go?
At the top right of the letter.
2. Name two good closings for an informal letter.
Love,  /  Your friend, (then your name). Avoid "Yours faithfully" — that's for formal letters.
7Remember this
✉️
"Address, Dear, News, Question, Love." Five parts, top to bottom — and always ask your friend something back.

Unit 9

On Top of the World

This unit celebrates achievers — like Pasindu, who got 3 A passes, and Jayanthi Kuru-Utumpala, the first Sri Lankan to climb Everest. The grammar tool you master here: adverbs of frequency. You also learn to describe an achiever.

1

Adverbs of frequencyalways · usually · often · sometimes · rarely · never

▾
1Let's get into it
How often do YOU do it? The star student Pasindu in your textbook gets up at 5 a.m. every single day. Do you? Always? Sometimes? Never? The little words that answer "how often?" are called adverbs of frequency, and they make your English sound natural and exact.
2In plain words

An adverb of frequency tells how often something happens. The Pupil's Book (p.99) gives six, from the most often to the least often:

always → usually (normally) → often → sometimes → rarely → never.

The big question students get wrong is where to put them. Two simple rules:

• Put the adverb before a normal (main) verb. → "She always walks to school."
• Put it after the verb be (am / is / are / was / were). → "She is always early."
• Phrases like once and twice go at the end. → "The truck comes once a week."

3See it — the frequency ladder
4Worked examples + your turn
From Pasindu (Activity 9.2)

He always comes to school early.  (before the main verb "comes")

He rarely gets absent. He never stays in bed later than 6.30.

Match the meaning (Workbook Act. 01)
always = at all times · usually = generally · often = many times
sometimes = now and then · rarely = not often · never = not at any time

✍️ Your turn (Workbook Act. 02): put the words in order — often / sleeps / under / his / dog / bed / Suren's

"Suren's dog often sleeps under his bed." — the adverb goes before the verb "sleeps."
I always tell the truth about how often I tell lies. 🤔
5Watch out!
Common mistakes
  • With am/is/are/was/were, the adverb goes after: She is always happy. (not She always is happy.)
  • With other verbs, it goes before: I never lie. (not I lie never.)
  • rarely and never are already negative — don't add another "not": I don't never…
6Quick self-check
⏱️ Slide to see which adverb fits how often you do something
1. Correct the sentence: "Chathura gets up always before 5.30."
"Chathura always gets up before 5.30." (adverb before the main verb).
2. Which word means "now and then"?
sometimes (about half the time).
7Remember this
📍
"Before the verb, but after 'be'." That single line places every frequency adverb correctly.

Writing: describe an achiever (& a paragraph about you)Activities 9.5 & 9.7

▾
1Let's get into it
From a village school to the top of the world. Jayanthi Kuru-Utumpala became the first Sri Lankan to reach the summit of Mount Everest (21 May 2016). Your textbook tells her story so you can learn to describe an achiever — and write about your own daily habits too.
2In plain words

A good description of a person follows a natural order. Turn fact-bullets into smooth sentences in this sequence:

born → education → dream → training → big achievement → awards / message.

3See it — the achiever timeline
4Worked example + your turn
Model — describing Jayanthi

Jayanthi Kuru-Utumpala was born in 1979 in Colombo. She studied at Bishop's College and later earned a degree in English Literature. She had always dreamed of climbing Everest. After tough training — rock-climbing, ice-climbing and survival skills — she became the first Sri Lankan to reach the summit of Everest on 21st May 2016.

✍️ Your turn (Activity 9.5): write 5 sentences about yourself, using always, usually, often, sometimes, rarely, never at least once each.

"I always get up at 5.30. I usually revise after school. I often play cricket in the evening. I sometimes help my mother cook, but I rarely skip breakfast and I never go to bed without reading."
5Watch out!
Common mistakes
  • Keep the time order — don't jump from "she won an award" back to "she was born."
  • Use the past tense for a finished life-story (was, studied, became), and place frequency adverbs correctly in your own paragraph.
6Quick self-check
1. Put in order: award · born · training · dream
born → dream → training → award (time order).
2. Improve: "She is a climber. She climbed Everest. She was born in 1979."
Re-order into time sequence: "She was born in 1979… she trained hard… and she climbed Everest." Smooth and logical.
7Remember this
🏔️
"Born, learned, dreamed, trained, achieved." Tell any achiever's story in time order and it almost writes itself.

Unit 10

Beyond the Classroom

Learning happens everywhere — at an English Day, on a boat with Nasrudin, in everyday life. The grammar tool here is the past perfect tense, plus how to use inverted commas and write a story with dialogue.

1

The Past Perfect Tensehad + past participle (V3)

▾
1Let's get into it
"It had already started when we got there." You arrive at the English Day a little late, and your friend says exactly that. Two things happened in the past — the show starting and you arriving — but one happened first. The tense for "the one that happened first" is the past perfect.
2In plain words

Form (Pupil's Book p.106): had + past participle (V3). Use it for a past action that happened before another past action.

The past participle (V3) is the third form of a verb: work → worked → worked; go → went → gone; write → wrote → written; leave → left → left.

FormPatternExample
Affirmativesubject + had + V3He had worked.
QuestionHad + subject + V3?Had he worked?
Negativesubject + had not (hadn't) + V3He had not worked.
3See it — the timeline
4Worked example + your turn
Worked

"She was happy because she had won a prize." → winning came first, so it is past perfect (had won); being happy came after.

✍️ Your turn (Activity 10.3): fill in the past perfect — 1) "Mr. Perera went back to the office. He ______ (leave) his spectacles behind." 2) "I enjoyed the play. I ______ (not / see) a stage play before."

1) had left   2) had not seen (hadn't seen). Both name something that happened before the main past event.
5Watch out!
Common mistakes
  • Use the past participle (V3), not the past simple: had gone (not had went), had written (not had wrote).
  • Use it only when there are two past times and you want to show which was earlier. For one simple past event, use the past simple.
  • The negative is had not / hadn't — never "didn't had."
6Quick self-check
⏪ Tap a verb to build its past perfect (had + V3)
Tap a verb above…
1. "By the time we arrived, the film ______ (start)."
had started — it started before we arrived.
2. Spot the error: "He had wrote the script."
"He had written the script." (use the past participle, not "wrote").
7Remember this
⏪
"Two past actions? The earlier one wears 'HAD'." had + V3 = the action that came first.
2

Past simple vs past perfect — which came first?Workbook Activity 03

▾
1Let's get into it
Two actions, one sentence. Real stories often pack two past actions into one sentence: "The visitor had lost his passport, so he had trouble at the airport." How do you know which tense goes where? Easy — by asking "which happened first?"
2In plain words

When a sentence has two past actions:

• the earlier action → past perfect (had + V3)
• the later action → past simple (the normal past).

3See it
4Worked example + your turn
Worked (Workbook Act. 03)

"You enjoyed the movie because you had read the book." → reading the book happened first (past perfect); enjoying came later (past simple).

✍️ Your turn: choose past simple or past perfect — 1) "After he ______ (eat) all the sandwiches, he ______ (drink) some juice." 2) "Sumudu found the test easy because he ______ (do) a similar test before."

1) "After he had eaten all the sandwiches, he drank some juice."   2) "…because he had done a similar test before."
5Watch out!
Common mistakes
  • Don't put both actions in the past perfect — only the earlier one gets "had."
  • Signal words like before, after, by the time, because often point to the earlier action.
6Quick self-check
1. "When I reached home, my mother ______ (cook) dinner already."
had cooked — cooking finished before I reached.
2. Which action is earlier? "She cried because she had failed."
had failed (past perfect) is earlier; crying came after.
7Remember this
⚖️
"First then second: HAD then did." The first event gets had; the second stays in the simple past.
3

Inverted commas (quotation marks)“ ” · Pupil's Book p.109

▾
1Let's get into it
"You've made a silly mistake." In the funny Nasrudin story, the proud traveller laughs and says exactly that. Those little marks — “ ” — wrapped around his words are inverted commas. They tell the reader: these are the exact words someone spoke.
2In plain words

Pupil's Book (p.109): use inverted commas (quotation marks) when you quote the exact words of a speaker or writer.

Three small rules:
• Put the spoken words inside the marks.
• Start them with a capital letter.
• The full stop / comma / question mark / exclamation mark goes inside the closing mark.

3See it

Full sentence: Malith asked, “What are you doing?”  (comma after "asked", capital W, "?" inside)

4Worked example + your turn
Worked

Surani lamented, “I think I made a lot of mistakes.”

✍️ Your turn (Workbook Act. 02): add inverted commas — Come on! cheered Malith.

“Come on!” cheered Malith. — the spoken words sit inside the marks, and the exclamation mark stays inside.
5Watch out!
Common mistakes
  • Only the exact spoken words go inside — not "he said" or "she asked."
  • Begin the speech with a capital letter, even in the middle of a sentence.
  • Tuck the punctuation inside the closing mark, not outside.
6Quick self-check
1. Add the marks: Why are you worrying? asked Malith.
“Why are you worrying?” asked Malith.
2. True or false: the comma after "said" goes inside the quotation marks.
False. "He said," — that comma is outside; only the speaker's own punctuation goes inside the marks.
7Remember this
❝
"Hug the spoken words with “ ”, and tuck the punctuation inside."
4

Writing: tell a story with dialogueActivity 10.6 · the English Day report

▾
1Let's get into it
Now put it all together in a story. Unit 10 asks you to write a story with conversation (Activity 10.6) and to describe an event like the English Day report (p.107). This is your chance to use the past perfect and inverted commas in one piece.
2In plain words

A good short story with dialogue needs three things:

• Past perfect for whatever happened earlier (had started, had practised).
• Inverted commas around each spoken line.
• A new line for each new speaker, so the reader follows who is talking.

3See it — the model
Model story

When we reached the hall, the English Day had already started. A grade 8 student had written the drama, and the cast had practised well.

“Shall we also do a drama next year?” asked Supun.

“Good idea,” replied Rahal. “Let's tell our English teacher.”

4Worked + your turn

The English Day report (p.107) answers: event, organised by, place, date, chief guest, items presented. A report is factual; a story is lively. Both can use the past perfect for things that happened first.

✍️ Your turn: write a 6-sentence story about an event you attended. Use two past-perfect verbs and two lines of dialogue with inverted commas.

"By the time I reached the ground, the match had begun. Our team had lost two early wickets. “Don't worry, we can still win!” shouted my friend. “Let's cheer louder,” I replied."
5Watch out!
Common mistakes
  • Don't run all the speech together — give each speaker a new line.
  • Keep tenses consistent: simple past for the main story, past perfect only for earlier actions.
6Quick self-check
1. Fix the dialogue: shall we go and play asked Pubudu
“Shall we go and play?” asked Pubudu. (capital S, "?" inside, marks around the speech)
2. Which verb is past perfect: "had practised" or "practised"?
had practised (had + V3). "practised" alone is past simple.
7Remember this
📖
"Earlier action → had + V3. New speaker → new line, in “ ”." That's a story examiners love.

⚡ One-page Cheat Sheet

Read just this in the last 5 minutes before an exam. (You can print it — printing also reveals all hidden answers.)

🪞 Reflexive pronouns

  • myself · yourself · himself · herself · itself
  • ourselves · yourselves · themselves
  • -self = one, -selves = many. Bounces back, or adds emphasis.

🔢 Plural count nouns

  • Most: add -s (cup → cups).
  • Hiss (s/ss/sh/ch/x) or consonant+o → -es (bus → buses, mango → mangoes).
  • consonant+y → -ies (city → cities). Singular needs a/an/the.

👅 /pl/ vs /pr/

  • /pl/ tongue flat: plan, play, plug.
  • /pr/ tongue curled: prince, proud, prize.
  • No vowel in between — never "pu-lan."

✉️ Informal letter

  • Address + date (top right) → "Dear ___," → opening → news → question back → "Love, name".
  • Commas after the greeting and the closing.

⏱️ Adverbs of frequency

  • always → usually → often → sometimes → rarely → never.
  • Before the main verb, after the verb "be".
  • once / twice → at the end of the sentence.

🏔️ Describe an achiever

  • Order: born → education → dream → training → achievement → award.
  • Use the past tense; keep the time order.

⏪ Past perfect

  • had + V3: the earlier of two past actions.
  • Q: Had he…?   Neg: hadn't…
  • V3 not V2: had gone/written, not "had went/wrote".

❝ ❞ Inverted commas & stories

  • Wrap the exact spoken words; capital first letter; punctuation inside.
  • New speaker → new line. Earlier action → had + V3.
