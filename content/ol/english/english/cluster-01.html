Why does this paper matter? 🎯

Question type 1 · easy marks

Picture & Word Matching

Look at a picture and choose the matching description or word. The first easy marks for everyone!

By the end of this section you will be able to 🎯
  • recognise words that describe appearance (face, hair, features)
  • match prepositions of place (next to, behind…) to pictures
  • match action / cooking verbs to pictures
1a Describing appearance ›
1 Hook
Imagine a friend has gone missing and you must describe them to the police — you'd need words like "round face, curly hair, a beard", right? Know these appearance words and this question is done in a minute!
2 The words (Pupil's Book — Unit 1 People / Unit 10 Personality)
CategoryEnglish words
Face shaperound, oval, square face
Hairstraight / wavy / curly / fair / black hair · bald
Other featuresbeard, moustache, pointed nose, sharp eyes, fair / dark complexion
She has an oval face with long wavy hair and sharp eyes.
Use has / has got when describing appearance.
5 Watch out!
⚠ Common mistake
Don't mix up "a beard" (on the chin) and "a moustache" (above the lip). And hair is uncountable — never write "hairs".

1b Prepositions of place ›
1 Hook
When you tell someone where the kade (shop) is — "next to the bank, opposite the temple" — you're using prepositions of place. Match these to map pictures.
3 See it — the cat and the box 🐱

5 Watch out!
⚠ Often confused
opposite (facing, across from) ≠ next to (beside). behind (at the back) ≠ in front of.

in/on/at/under = one object; next to/opposite/between = relative to another object.

1 Hook
When amma cooks — "slice the onions, fry the fish, knead the dough". Match these verbs to recipe pictures! (Pupil's Book — Unit 8 Healthy Food)

Question type 2 · easy marks

Dialogue Gap-Fill

Complete the missing lines of a conversation. This tests everyday spoken English.

By the end of this section you will be able to 🎯
  • choose polite/appropriate phrases to fit the context
  • use short-answer agreements (So do I / Neither have I) correctly
  • use the line after the blank as a clue
2a Agreement short answers — "So do I / Neither do I" ›
1 Hook
Friend: "I love cricket." You: "So do I!" These little replies appear in the exam every year — and many students get them wrong. Learn the trick = instant marks!
2 The secret: keep the auxiliary from the first sentence
First sentenceIf you agree
I like tea. (positive)So do I.
I don't like tea. (negative)Neither do I.
I have finished.So have I.
I haven't finished.Neither have I.
I can swim.So can I.
📏 The rule: For a positive sentence → So + (auxiliary) + I. For a negative sentence → Neither + (auxiliary) + I. Take the auxiliary (do/have/can/am…) straight from the first sentence!
✏️ Your turn: A: "I don't eat meat." → B?
Show answer
Neither do I. (negative sentence → Neither + do + I)
🎮 Choose the short answer
5 Watch out!
⚠ Don't change the auxiliary
For simple-past statements like "I went…" → So did I (do→did). "I am happy." → So am I. Keep the same tense/auxiliary as the first sentence.
🔑
Positive → So; Negative → Neither. Auxiliary from the first sentence + I.
2b Register & context ›
2 Match the language to the setting (Pupil's Book — Unit 4 Let's talk / Unit 11 The right career)

The words change with the place: a hotel, an interview, a shop, or between friends.

(Interview) "Certainly, sir. I worked as a trainee for two years."
(Interview) "Yeah, whatever, two years or so."
(Hotel) Receptionist: "Good morning. How may I help you?"   Guest: "I'd like to check in, please."
Polite phrases: Could you…?, May I…?, I'd like…, Certainly, Thank you.
6 Quick self-check
✅ Quick self-check
  1. What should you do before filling a blank?
    Answer
    Read the whole dialogue — especially the line after the blank; it usually hints what fits.
🔑
Read the line after the blank. Match the politeness level (a teacher ≠ a friend).

Question type 3 · the most learnable!

Grammar in Context

Correct the errors, or fill blanks with the right form — tense, articles, prepositions, agreement.

By the end of this section you will be able to 🎯
  • use the four past tenses correctly (Pupil's Book p.11)
  • handle articles, prepositions and subject–verb agreement
  • form words using prefixes and suffixes
⭐ Most important tip: This is the most learnable part of the whole paper. Tense and subject–verb agreement come up every single year — practise past papers here!
3a Tenses — Pupil's Book Revision point, p.11 ›
1 Hook
A tense is like a time machine ⏰ — the verb changes to show when something happened. To write about the past, English has four past tenses.
2 The four past tenses (with the book's own examples)
TenseUseExample (PB p.11)
Simple Pasta single action at a point in the pastOn my last birthday, I donated some books.
Past Continuousan action going on at a point in the pastI was waiting for a letter when the postman delivered it.
Past Perfectan action completed before another past actionMy father had visited the library before he organized this.
Past Perfect Continuousan action that began in the past and went onHe had been waiting for a long time to get those books.
By the time we arrived, the train left.
By the time we arrived, the train had left.
✏️ Your turn: "While I ___ (cook), the phone rang."
Show answer
was cooking (an action going on → Past Continuous)
🔑
The earlier past action → had + V3 (Past Perfect). An ongoing action → was/were + V-ing.
3b Articles (a / an / the) ›
2 Simple rules
a — before a consonant sound (a boy, a university). an — before a vowel sound (an apple, an hour). the — for something specific or already mentioned (the sun, the book I bought).
She is a honest girl. · I saw a elephant.
She is an honest girl. · I saw an elephant.
⚠ Listen to the sound, not the letter!
an hour (the "h" is silent → vowel sound); a university (the "yu" → consonant sound). Sound, not spelling!
🔑
Vowel sound → an; consonant sound → a; specific thing → the.
3c Prepositions (in / on / at …) ›
2 For time — AT / ON / IN
at + a clock time (at 5 o'clock). on + a day/date (on Monday, on April 14th). in + a month/year (in May, in 2026).
We celebrate Avurudu in April, on the 14th, at the auspicious time.
⚠ since vs for
since = from a point in time (since 2015); for = a length of time (for three years).
🔑
Smallest → AT (time); medium → ON (day); largest → IN (month/year).
3d Subject–Verb Agreement ›
2 The rule

A singular subject takes a singular verb (He plays); a plural subject takes a plural verb (They play).

She have finished. · The students was happy.
She has finished. · The students were happy.
⚠ Irregular plurals
criterion→criteria, medium→media, syllabus→syllabi — these are plural! "The criteria are strict." (not "is").
🔑
First decide if the subject is singular or plural → match the verb.
3e Word formation — Prefixes & Suffixes (PB Revision point) ›

Effectchanges the meaning (usually not the word-class)often changes the word-class
e.g.in-, un-, im-, ir-, dis-, mis-, re--ize, -ful, -less, -ness, -ly, -tion, -ment, -ous

A suffix changes the word's "job" (word-class).
✏️ Your turn: "She answered with great ___ (confident)."
Show answer
confidence (a noun is needed here → confident → confidence)
🎮 Choose the correct form (Grammar)
7 Memory hook
🔑
Prefix = changes meaning (un-/dis- = "not"); Suffix = changes word-class (-ly→adverb, -ness→noun).
3f Modals · Reported speech · Conjunctions (PB) ›
2 Quick summary
PointRule / example
Modals (advice/obligation)should, must, mustn't, ought to · "You should revise daily."
Reported speechtense shifts back one step · "I am tired" → He said he was tired.
Conjunctionsalthough, because, while, so, but · "Although it rained, we played."
⚠ Reported-speech backshift
say + that; tell + (someone) + that. Shift the tense back: am→was, like→liked, will→would, today→that day.
🔑
Reported: tense back + change pronouns + today→that day.

Question type 4 · medium

Reading Comprehension

Read a passage and answer questions. Some answers are direct; some need your own words.

By the end of this section you will be able to 🎯
  • use the question's keyword to find the answer in the passage
  • identify pronoun reference (it/they/this)
  • answer "in your own words" without copying
4a Text types & finding answers ›
1 Hook
Under a newspaper headline there's small text with the details, right? Notices, newspaper articles, and autobiographies all work like that — practise reading them and pulling out the key facts.
2 The golden method (3 steps)
① Underline the keyword in the question. ② Find that word (or its meaning) in the passage. ③ Write your answer based on that sentence.
Nimal bought a new phone. It was very expensive.
Here It = the new phone. (A pronoun — look back to the noun before it!)
5 Watch out!
⚠ "In your own words" — don't copy!
If the question says "in your own words" and you copy the passage exactly, you lose the mark. Rewrite the meaning (paraphrase) — use synonyms (big→large, happy→glad).
6 Quick self-check
✅ Quick self-check
  1. "The students cheered. They were happy." — They = ?
    Answer
    the students.
  2. What do you do for an "own words" question?
    Answer
    Paraphrase the meaning — don't copy.
🔑
Keyword → find it in the passage. "Own words" = paraphrase, never copy.

Putting it all together — exam strategy 🏆

The smart order to attempt Paper I:

1 Picture matching→2 Dialogue→3 Grammar→5 Info transfer→4 Reading

• Easy marks first (picture, dialogue) — build confidence and bank marks.
• Grammar — most marks for your practice; tense + agreement come every year.
• Reading last — it takes the most time, so protect your other marks first.
• The golden rule: never leave a blank — there's no negative marking!
