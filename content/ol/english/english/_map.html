O/L English — The Whole Paper

English isn't a subject you memorise. It's a paper you can predict. The same task types come back every year, in the same order. Once you know the shape of the paper and exactly what each task wants, the fear goes. Here's the whole paper, laid out plainly. Come and see for yourself.

The mindset that changes your marks. Most students lose marks not because their English is weak, but because they don't know what each task is asking for — they write a letter when a notice was wanted, or 200 words when 50 were enough. English rewards doing the right thing in the right format, not showing off vocabulary. Learn the format of each task below, and you collect marks the strong-English students leave behind.

Paper 1 · The Test Paper — shorter answers, fill-ins ▶

Picture & word matching Easy marks

Match pictures to descriptions, or words to meanings. The opening task, designed so everyone starts with marks on the board.

Tip: Never leave a blank. Even a guess by elimination scores — there is no negative marking. Do this first, fast, then move on with confidence.

Dialogue gap-fill Easy marks

Complete the missing lines in a conversation, often by choosing the right option. Tests everyday spoken English.

Tip: Read the whole dialogue first before filling any blank — the line after the gap usually tells you what fits. Match the politeness level (a reply to a teacher isn't the same as to a friend).

Grammar in context (correct the errors / choose the word)

A short passage with underlined errors to fix, or blanks to fill with the correct grammatical form — tense, article, preposition, agreement.

Tip: This is the most learnable part of the whole paper. Tense and subject–verb agreement repeat every year. Drilling past papers here lifts marks faster than anything else.

Reading comprehension Medium weight

Read a passage, answer questions on it. Some answers are stated directly; some need you to infer or explain in your own words.

Tip: Underline the question's key word, then find that word (or its meaning) in the passage. For "in your own words" questions, copying the passage exactly often loses the mark — you must reword.

Information transfer (notice → form, table → sentences)

Take information from one format and put it into another — read a notice and fill a form, or read a table and write sentences.

Tip: Answer only what's asked. Extra invented detail can cost marks. Copy names, dates and numbers exactly as given — spelling errors here are avoidable losses.

Paper 2 · The Writing Paper — where the big marks live ▶

Guided / short writing — notice, message, announcement Medium weight

Write a short functional text to a word limit: a notice, a short message, an invitation, an announcement. Format matters as much as the English.

Tip: Each text type has a fixed shape examiners look for — a notice has a heading, date and signature; a message is brief and direct. Learn the five or six shapes and you never lose format marks. Stay near the word limit — far over or under is penalised.

Letter writing — formal & informal High weight

Write a letter to a given situation. Could be informal (to a friend) or formal (to an editor, a principal, an official). The format differs sharply between the two.

Tip: The single most common mark-loss in the whole exam: mixing formal and informal. Decide which it is in the first ten seconds, then keep the greeting, tone and sign-off consistent. Memorise both formats cold.

Paragraph & essay / composition Highest weight

The biggest single block of marks. Write a paragraph or short essay on a given topic — often a choice of several. Tests ideas, organisation and language together.

Tip: Plan for two minutes before writing — three or four points in order beats a flood of unstructured sentences. A clear beginning, middle and end scores higher than fancy words in a jumble. Choose the topic you have ideas for, not the one that sounds impressive.

Speaking & Listening — the school-based / practical component ▶

Listening tasks

Listen to a short text — an announcement, a conversation, instructions — and answer questions: specific information like dates, times, names, places.

Tip: Read the questions before the audio starts, so you know what to listen for. You're hunting for specific facts, not understanding every word.

Speaking tasks

Speak on a topic, describe a picture, or hold a short conversation. Assessed on fluency and getting your meaning across — not a perfect accent.

Tip: Confidence and keeping going matter more than big words. If you pause, use a filler ("Well…", "Let me think…") instead of stopping. Use words you're sure of.

Important — check your marks against a real past paper. Task types and their order are stable year to year, but exact mark weights and which part each task falls in can shift slightly between exam years. Before relying on the "weight" labels above, open your most recent Department of Examinations O/L English past paper and confirm.
